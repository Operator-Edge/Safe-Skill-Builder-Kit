**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Write your research topic at the top of a blank document. Be specific. Instead of "AI tools," write "AI tools for small business owners who aren't technical." Specific topics produce better research.

2. Write three questions you want to answer by the end of your research. These are your anchors — they keep the research focused and prevent you from going in circles.

3. **Read Layer 1 — General Overview:**
   Search for your topic on a general search engine. Read the descriptions and titles of the first page of results without clicking into every link. Write down: What's the general shape of this topic? What subtopics come up most often?

4. **Read Layer 2 — What People Are Asking:**
   Search your topic with the word "question" or "how do I" or "what is the best" added. Look at the questions that come up in search suggestions or "people also ask" sections. Write down the 3 to 5 most common questions people have about this topic.

5. **Read Layer 3 — What People Are Saying:**
   Find one or two discussion threads (Reddit, forums, community sites) about your topic. Read them. Write down: What problems come up most? What do people recommend? What do they warn against?

6. **Read Layer 4 — What Already Exists:**
   Search for existing solutions to the problem your topic is about. Write down: What options exist? Are they free or paid? What do users say is good and bad about them?

7. Review your notes from all four layers. Look for answers to your three research questions. Write the answers in plain language, even if they're partial.

8. Write your structured output using this format:

   **Topic:** [Your research topic]

   **What I Learned (Key Findings):**
   — [Finding 1]
   — [Finding 2]
   — [Finding 3]

   **Common Problems People Have:**
   — [Problem 1]
   — [Problem 2]

   **What Currently Exists:**
   — [Solution 1 and brief note]
   — [Solution 2 and brief note]

   **Gaps or Opportunities:**
   — [What's missing or could be better]

   **My Summary:**
   [Two to three sentences summarizing the most important thing you found]

9. Read your structured output. Does it answer your three research questions? If not, note which question needs more research and run one more targeted search.

10.

**Example Usage:**

Input: Topic — "Note-taking apps for people who lose track of ideas"
Research questions:
1. What apps are most popular for capturing ideas quickly?
2. What frustrates people most about existing apps?
3. Is there anything no current app does well?

Output (abbreviated):

**Topic:** Note-taking apps for idea capture — non-technical users

**Key Findings:**
— Notion, Obsidian, and Apple Notes dominate discussions
— Most frustration centers on complexity — people want to capture fast, not organize
— Voice-to-text capture is highly requested but poorly implemented in most apps

**Common Problems:**
— Apps require setup before you can use them
— Sync issues between devices

**What Exists:**
— Notion: powerful but complex — often "too much"
— Apple Notes: simple but lacks organization features

**Gaps:**
— An app (or skill) focused purely on fast, zero-setup idea capture with optional organization later

**Summary:** The idea-capture space is busy but underserved for simplicity-first users. People want to record a thought in seconds without configuring anything first.
